* Solution

1. The geographical scatter plot colors districts by median house value
   and sizes them by population. What patterns do you notice, and what
   does the plot reveal that a table of numbers cannot?

   #+begin_quote
   The plot reveals three things simultaneously that no table can:
   /where/ districts are, /how big/ they are (dot size = population), and
   /how expensive/ they are (color). The pattern that jumps out: high
   house values (red/warm) cluster along the California coast — the Bay
   Area and the LA basin — while inland districts are predominantly
   blue. Population and price co-localize in the same coastal urban
   centers. The spatial clustering makes the =ocean_proximity= feature
   feel almost redundant — you can see the gradient by eye. A table of
   longitude, latitude, and price numbers would take significant mental
   work to produce this insight; the plot produces it in one glance.
   #+end_quote

2. Géron computes a correlation matrix and identifies ~median_income~ as
   the strongest predictor of ~median_house_value~ with a coefficient of
   0.69. What does this number mean in plain English, and what does it
   *not* tell you?

   #+begin_quote
   In plain English: districts with higher median incomes tend to have
   substantially higher median house values, and this tendency is fairly
   consistent across the data. More precisely, when income goes up by
   one standard deviation, house value tends to go up by about 0.69
   standard deviations on average.

   What it does /not/ tell you: (1) *Causation* — high income and high
   house prices likely share a common cause (desirable location, good
   schools) rather than one causing the other. (2) *Non-linear
   structure* — the zoomed scatter plot shows noise and clear artifacts
   (horizontal lines at $450k and $350k, hard cap at $500k) that
   distort the coefficient downward. (3) *Explained variance* — 0.69²
   ≈ 0.48, so income alone accounts for roughly half the variance in
   house value; the other half comes from everything else.
   #+end_quote

3. Géron engineers three new features from existing ones
   (~rooms_per_house~, ~bedrooms_ratio~, ~people_per_house~) and checks their
   correlations with ~median_house_value~. What do you find, and why do
   you think that might be?

   #+begin_quote
   The raw counts (=total_rooms=, =total_bedrooms=, =population=) are
   district-level totals that conflate district size with housing
   character. Dividing by households normalizes for this:

   | Feature            | Approx. correlation with =median_house_value= |
   |--------------------+-----------------------------------------------|
   | =rooms_per_house=    | ~+0.14                                        |
   | =bedrooms_ratio=     | ~−0.26                                        |
   | =people_per_house=   | ~−0.04                                        |

   =bedrooms_ratio= is the most revealing: a /lower/ ratio (fewer bedrooms
   relative to total rooms) correlates with /higher/ prices. Expensive
   homes tend to have more living space, hallways, studies, and
   open-plan areas relative to the number of bedrooms — a cramped
   apartment block has a high bedroom ratio; a spacious single-family
   home does not. =rooms_per_house= also beats =total_rooms= (which was
   near zero) because per-household room count is a proxy for home
   size, not just district density. The broader lesson: raw counts in
   this dataset are confounded by how many households share them —
   ratios remove that confound and expose the underlying signal.
   #+end_quote
