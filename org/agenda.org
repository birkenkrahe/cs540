*** Chapter 1: What is Machine Learning?

1. You work at a city transportation authority with five years of bus
   ridership data (time of day, route, weather, local events). Your
   manager asks for two things: a system that predicts tomorrow's
   passenger load on each route, and an analysis of which factors most
   strongly drive ridership spikes on game days. What distinction in
   ML tasks is your manager describing — and why might the same
   dataset serve both goals but require different framing?

2. You work at a streaming music company. You have ten million user
   listening histories but no genre labels — users never categorize
   what they play. Your product team wants to group users by taste so
   the app can suggest new artists. Is this a supervised or unsupervised
   problem? Why can't you just train a genre classifier instead, and
   what does this reveal about the role of labeled training data?

3. You work at an insurance company building a model to predict whether
   a customer will file a claim within the next year. A colleague builds
   the model, evaluates it on the same 200,000 records used for
   training, and reports 98% accuracy. Why should you be skeptical of
   this result — and which step of the ML workflow did they skip?

*** Chapter 2: Machine Learning Models

1. You work at a hospital with 50,000 patient records: blood test
   results, age, BMI, and whether each patient was diagnosed with Type 2
   diabetes within three years. Your team wants to flag high-risk
   patients for early intervention. Is this classification or
   regression? What is the target variable, what are candidate features,
   and why does the framing matter for choosing a model?

2. You work at a retail chain with two million loyalty-card transactions
   but no pre-defined customer segments. Marketing wants to launch
   targeted promotions. You have no labels and no prior knowledge of how
   many customer types exist. What ML approach do you use, and what
   challenge will you face in interpreting whatever the algorithm
   discovers?

3. You work at a bank deploying a fraud detection model. It achieves 97%
   accuracy on the test set, but your risk team is alarmed — the model
   is missing the majority of actual fraudulent transactions. Why is
   accuracy misleading here? Which metric should you optimize instead,
   and what does a low sensitivity score tell you in practical terms?

*** Chapter 3: Deep Learning

1. You work at a small nonprofit with 3,000 labeled medical intake forms
   (structured fields: age, symptoms, diagnosis code). Your intern
   recommends a deep learning model "because it's more powerful." You
   have a standard laptop and a $200/month cloud GPU budget. What is
   your recommendation, and which four criteria from the course should
   guide the decision of when to use deep learning?

2. You work at a manufacturing company and want to automatically detect
   defects in circuit boards from photos taken on the assembly line. You
   have 500,000 labeled images (defective / not defective) at 1024×1024
   resolution in grayscale. How many features does one image produce?
   Why is a neural network well-suited here, and what do early vs. late
   hidden layers typically learn to detect?

3. You work at an HR software company. Your résumé-screening model was
   trained on ten years of historical hiring decisions and consistently
   downranks female applicants. A civil rights audit flags it for bias.
   What went wrong at the data quality level — and what does the
   "garbage in, garbage out" principle mean in this context?
