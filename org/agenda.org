*** Chapter 1: What is Machine Learning?

1. You work at a city transportation authority with five years of bus
   ridership data (time of day, route, weather, local events). Your
   manager asks for two things: a system that predicts tomorrow's
   passenger load on each route, and an analysis of which factors most
   strongly drive ridership spikes on game days. What distinction in
   ML tasks is your manager describing — and why might the same
   dataset serve both goals but require different framing?

   #+begin_notes
   1) Prediction: future value - what's tomorrow's load on route 12.
   2) Inference: causal insight - which features explain spikes?
   3) Same data, different outputs.
   #+end_notes

2. You work at a streaming music company. You have ten million user
   listening histories but no genre labels — users never categorize
   what they play. Your product team wants to group users by taste so
   the app can suggest new artists. Is this a supervised or unsupervised
   problem? Why can't you just train a genre classifier instead, and
   what does this reveal about the role of labeled training data?

   #+begin_notes
   1) Unsupervised. No genre labels available.
   2) Without labels, you can only cluster data.
   #+end_notes

3. You work at an insurance company building a model to predict whether
   a customer will file a claim within the next year. A colleague builds
   the model, evaluates it on the same 200,000 records used for
   training, and reports 98% accuracy. Why should you be skeptical of
   this result — and which step of the ML workflow did they skip?

   #+begin_notes
   The model has not been tested on new data - it's been overfitted
   and simply memorized the data - it cannot generalize. The dataset
   needs to be split into separate training and testing sets.
   #+end_notes

*** Chapter 2: Machine Learning Models

1. You work at a hospital with 50,000 patient records: blood test
   results, age, BMI, and whether each patient was diagnosed with Type 2
   diabetes within three years. Your team wants to flag high-risk
   patients for early intervention. Is this classification or
   regression? What is the target variable, what are candidate features,
   and why does the framing matter for choosing a model?

   #+begin_notes
   1) Classification.
   2) Target: binary (intervention yes/no)
   3) All other features are candidate features.
   4) Classification models produce class membership, regression
      models produce continuous numbers.
   #+end_notes

2. You work at a retail chain with two million loyalty-card transactions
   but no pre-defined customer segments. Marketing wants to launch
   targeted promotions. You have no labels and no prior knowledge of how
   many customer types exist. What ML approach do you use, and what
   challenge will you face in interpreting whatever the algorithm
   discovers?

   #+begin_notes
   1) Unsupervised learning to discover customer segments.
   2) Challenge: clusters will not be named or explained.
   #+end_notes

3. You work at a bank deploying a fraud detection model. It achieves 97%
   accuracy on the test set, but your risk team is alarmed — the model
   is missing the majority of actual fraudulent transactions. Why is
   accuracy misleading here? Which metric should you optimize instead,
   and what does a low sensitivity score tell you in practical terms?

   #+begin_notes
   1) Accuracy TP+TN/(TP+TN+FP+FN) is misleading because fraud is rare,
      so TN > FN but both are large, while TP (actual fraud) < FP
      (false alarms) is small.
   2) Sensitivity/recall TP/(TP+FN) will be near zero because the
      model does not find many actual fraud cases, and FN (missed
      fraud) dominates the output.
   3) A low sensitivity score means that most fraudulent transactions
      are classified as legitimate.

   The 3rd important measure is precision TP/(TP+FP), which measures
   how many of the model's positive predictions are correct.
   #+end_notes

*** Chapter 3: Deep Learning

1. You work at a small nonprofit with 3,000 labeled medical intake forms
   (structured fields: age, symptoms, diagnosis code). Your intern
   recommends a deep learning model "because it's more powerful." You
   have a standard laptop and a $200/month cloud GPU budget. What is
   your recommendation, and which four criteria from the course should
   guide the decision of when to use deep learning?

   #+begin_notes
   *Recommendation: use traditional ML* (logistic regression or gradient
   boosting). Deep learning requires all four of the following — none are
   met here: (1) *large data* — 3,000 records is too few; (2) *significant
   compute* — a laptop and $200/month GPU are insufficient for large
   networks; (3) *unstructured inputs* — structured form fields do not
   benefit from automatic feature learning; (4) *complex problem type* —
   medical form classification is well within classical ML's capability.
   #+end_notes

2. You work at a manufacturing company and want to automatically detect
   defects in circuit boards from photos taken on the assembly line. You
   have 500,000 labeled images (defective / not defective) at 1024×1024
   resolution in grayscale. How many features does one image produce?
   Why is a neural network well-suited here, and what do early vs. late
   hidden layers typically learn to detect?

   #+begin_notes
   1024 × 1024 × 1 (grayscale) = *1,048,576 features* per image. A neural
   network is well-suited because the feature engineering (what
   constitutes a defect) would be impossibly complex to hand-code. *Early
   hidden layers* learn low-level features (edges, textures); *later layers*
   combine these into higher-level patterns (shapes, structural
   anomalies) that distinguish defects from intact boards. The network
   figures out the features itself.
   #+end_notes

3. A résumé-screening model at an HR software company was trained on
   ten years of historical hiring decisions. It consistently scores
   male applicants higher than female applicants. A civil rights audit
   flags the model. Analyze what went wrong — if anything — at the
   data level, and explain what "garbage in, garbage out" does and
   does not mean in this context.

   #+begin_notes
   The training data accurately records a decade of real hiring
   decisions — there is no technical data quality violation in the
   standard sense (noise, corruption, missing values). Whether a
   problem exists depends on which of three distinct issues is at
   stake. First, a genuine data quality argument: if the intended
   target is job performance, historical hiring decisions are a
   questionable proxy, since they reflect not only candidate quality
   but managerial preferences and assumptions — here "garbage in,
   garbage out" legitimately applies. Second, a legal issue: in many
   jurisdictions, using sex as a hiring criterion (even implicitly) is
   prohibited regardless of whether the historical pattern was
   real. Third, a normative policy goal: if the aim is to change
   historical patterns, past decisions are counterproductive as
   training data — but this rests on a contested policy choice, not a
   neutral technical finding. These three issues are distinct and
   should not be collapsed into a single "bad data" diagnosis.
   #+end_notes

** DataCamp review questions (Understanding ML/Classification/Regression)

1. Why is 97% accuracy misleading for a fraud detection model?
   #+begin_quote
   Because fraud is rare, a model predicting "not fraud" for every
   case (producing only TN and FN, never TP) achieves high accuracy
   while catching zero fraud. Accuracy = (TP+TN)/(TP+TN+FP+FN) is
   useless on imbalanced datasets — use recall (aka sensitivity)
   instead: TP/(TP+FN).
   #+end_quote

2. How does a "regression ensemble" combine predictions?
   #+begin_quote
   It averages them. Three models predicting 3, 5, and 4 days yield
   (3+5+4)/3 = 4 days. Averaging reduces variance (spread of
   predictions across models) by canceling independent errors out.
   #+end_quote

3. How does a face recognition network learn across its layers?
   #+begin_quote
   1) Low-level features to detect edges and color transitions.
   2) Combination of features into shapes (eyes and noses).
   3) Recognition of whole faces.
   #+end_quote

4. "Unsupervised learning uncovers hidden patterns in unlabeled
   data" - but what if there are no hidden patterns? Can you come up
   with an example?
   #+begin_quote
   "A map is not the territory it represents, but, if correct, it
   has a similar structure to the territory, which accounts for its
   usefulness."[fn:1]

   *Example:* Identified 'customer' clusters can just be geometric
   patterns of noise. Marketing needs to verify the output.
   #+end_quote

5. What is needed to prepare data before training a supervised
   machine learning model? Difference to unsupervised learning?
   #+begin_quote
   The data must be preprocessed: *missing values eliminated,* features
   encoded in *numeric format*, and data *stored in arrays* or *data
   frames*. Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is recommended to
   understand distributions and spot problems. These preprocessing
   steps apply to unsupervised learning as well — the difference is
   that supervised data also requires labels paired with each input.
   #+end_quote

6. Why is the argument to ~model.fit()~ written as ~X,y~? What are these
   for example for a spam classifier?
   #+begin_quote
   Because ~X~ is a 2D matrix where each row is a training instance
   and each column is a feature, while ~y~ is a 1D vector of target
   labels, one per instance. The convention ~X,y~ reflects the
   mathematical notation for inputs and outputs in supervised
   learning.

   For a spam classifier, ~X~ could be a 6×2 matrix of six emails,
   each described by two features (e.g., number of "!" and number of
   mentions of "Urgent"). ~y~ would then be a vector of six labels:
   [spam, spam, ham, ham, spam, ham].
   #+end_quote

7. In kNN, the algorithm finds the k nearest neighbors to a a new data
   point. What does "nearest" mean and how is this quality computed?
   #+begin_quote
   "Nearest" means smallest distance in the feature space. This can
   for example be computed as Euclidean distance (square root of the
   sum over the differences squared for each feature), or as cosine
   similarity, which measures the angle between vectors rather than
   the distance between the endpoints. (There are many more measures).
   #+end_quote
   #+caption: cosine similarity illustrated (see home assignment)
   #+attr_html: :width 600px :float nil:
   [[../img/cosine_similarity_2d.svg]]

8. ~train_test_split~ has a ~stratify=y~ parameter. What does it do
   and when would you use it?
   #+begin_quote
   ~stratify=y~ ensures that the proportion of class labels in the
   original dataset is preserved in both the training and test sets.
   For example, if 20% of the data is labelled "fraud" and 80% is
   "not fraud", both splits will have that same 20/80 ratio. Use it
   whenever the target classes are imbalanced — without it, a small
   class could be underrepresented or absent in one of the splits,
   making evaluation unreliable.
   #+end_quote

9. In kNN, what is the effect of increasing k — and what are the
   risks at the two extremes (very low k vs. very high k)?
   #+begin_quote
   Increasing k smooths the decision boundary by averaging over more
   neighbors. At *very low k* (e.g. k=1) the model overfits: the
   boundary is jagged and sensitive to noise, memorizing individual
   training points. At *very high k* (e.g. k=50) the model underfits:
   the boundary is too smooth and fails to capture the true pattern in
   the data. The best k balances these extremes — in the churn dataset
   example from the course, peak test accuracy occurred at k=13.
   #+end_quote
   #+caption: Overfitting vs best fit vs underfitting
   #+attr_html: :width 800px :float nil:
   [[../img/knn_complexity.png]]

10. What is the purpose of ~random_state=42~ in ~train_test_split~,
    and what happens if you omit it?
    #+begin_quote
    ~random_state~ sets a seed for the random number generator that
    shuffles the data before splitting. Using the same seed guarantees
    that you get the exact same split every time you run the code,
    making results reproducible. If you omit it, the split is
    different on every run, so accuracy scores will vary and results
    cannot be reliably compared or shared.
    #+end_quote

Supervised Learning with scikit-learn - Reflection Questions

1. How do you decide whether a problem is regression or
   classification, and what changes in your code once you've decided?

   #+BEGIN_QUOTE
   Task type is diagnosed from the *target variable*: continuous numeric
   target -> regression; discrete/categorical target ->
   classification. This changes the choice of *estimator*
   (e.g. ~LinearRegression()~ vs ~LogisticRegression()~), the *evaluation*
   metric (R^2/MAE/RMSE vs accuracy/precision/recall/F1/AUC), and
   sometimes the data preprocessing (e.g. encoding the target for
   classification with ~OneHotEncoder()~).
   #+END_QUOTE

2. Your model gets 0.95 on train, 0.60 on test. Name what's wrong,
   name the fix, and say one sentence about why ~Lasso()~ and ~Ridge()~
   fix it differently.

   #+BEGIN_QUOTE
   This gap is *overfitting*: the model has memorized noise/structure
   specific to the training set that doesn't generalize. The fix is
   *regularization* (Ridge or Lasso), which penalizes large coefficients
   to reduce model complexity. Ridge (L2) shrinks all coefficients
   toward zero but rarely to exactly zero, because its constraint
   region is a circle; Lasso (L1) can set coefficients to exactly
   zero, because its constraint region is a diamond with corners where
   the loss ellipse is likely to first touch - giving Lasso built-in
   feature selection that Ridge doesn't have.
   #+END_QUOTE

3. You're evaluating a regression model and a classification
   model. Why can't you use R^2 for both, and what would you use
   instead for the classifier?

   #+BEGIN_QUOTE
   R^2 measures how much variance in a continuous target is explained
   by the model - it's defined in terms of squared differences from a
   mean, which only makes sense for continuous, ordered outputs. A
   classifier's target is categorical (discrete classes), so "variance
   explained" doesn't apply. Instead, classifiers are evaluated with
   metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, F1, or AUC - chosen based
   on what matters for the problem (e.g. accuracy for balanced
   classes, precision/recall/F1/AUC when classes are imbalanced or the
   cost of false positives/negatives differs).
   #+END_QUOTE

4. What's the difference between what ~cross_val_score~ does and what
   ~GridSearchCV~ does - aren't they both just running the model
   multiple times?

   #+BEGIN_QUOTE
   ~cross_val_score~ evaluates a single, already-chosen model /
   hyperparameter setting by running k-fold cross-validation and
   returning k scores (usually averaged) - it answers "how good is
   this one configuration?" ~GridSearchCV~ performs model selection: it
   tries every combination of hyperparameters in a specified grid,
   runs cross-validation on each combination, and returns the
   combination with the best average CV score (via .best_params_ and
   .best_score_). So cross_val_score is an evaluation tool;
   GridSearchCV is a search tool that uses cross-validation internally
   as its evaluation step for each candidate.

   One catch: ~GridSearchCV~'s cost grows fast - total fits = (number of
   hyperparameters) x (values per hyperparameter) x (CV folds), which
   can hit the hundreds even for a modest grid. ~RandomizedSearchCV~
   fixes this by sampling a fixed number of random combinations
   instead of trying all of them, trading exhaustiveness for speed.
   #+END_QUOTE

5. Why wrap preprocessing and the model together in a ~Pipeline~ instead
   of just calling ~.fit_transform()~ on your data and then fitting the
   model separately?

   #+BEGIN_QUOTE
   A Pipeline guarantees the exact same preprocessing steps
   (imputation, encoding, scaling, etc.) are applied consistently to
   training and test data, fit only on training data and then just
   transformed on test data - preventing *data leakage*
   (e.g. accidentally fitting a scaler on the full dataset, which lets
   test-set statistics leak into training). It also means calling
   ~.predict()~ or ~.score()~ on raw, untransformed data automatically
   pushes it through the full transform chain before it reaches the
   model, so there's no risk of forgetting a step or applying steps in
   the wrong order.
   #+END_QUOTE
